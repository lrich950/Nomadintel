Executive Summary

Mexico continues to be the most popular destination for North American digital nomads, with an estimated 1.6 million U.S. citizens living in the country. However, the landscape has shifted significantly under President Claudia Sheinbaum's administration (October 2024-present), with new policies affecting foreign residents, evolving security dynamics, and important changes to visa enforcement.

Key Takeaway: Mexico remains viable for remote workers, but the era of loose enforcement is ending. Proper visa compliance and location selection are now critical.

Current Administration

President Claudia Sheinbaum (MORENA party) took office in October 2024, continuing many of AMLO's policies while bringing her own technocratic approach. Key developments:

October 2024: Sheinbaum inaugurated with strong mandate (59% of vote)
January 2025: New "Digital Nomad Visa" program announced (6-month initial implementation)
March 2025: Increased enforcement against "perpetual tourists" working on tourist visas

What This Means for You

  • Visa enforcement is tightening: Immigration officials at major airports (Mexico City, Cancun, Guadalajara) are now asking more questions about employment and length of stay
  • New visa options emerging: The government is developing a formal digital nomad visa (expected launch Q2 2025) requiring proof of remote income
  • Nationalism rising: Growing sentiment around foreign gentrification, particularly in Mexico City and Oaxaca, leading to some anti-foreigner protests

üîí Security Situation

National Overview

Mexico's security landscape remains highly localized. While homicide rates have plateaued nationally, cartel violence continues in specific regions, and targeting of foreigners remains rare but not unknown.

Regional Breakdown

‚úÖ LOW RISK ZONES (Suitable for Digital Nomads)

  • Yucatan Peninsula: Merida, Playa del Carmen (selective areas), Tulum (selective areas) - Tourist economy creates security incentive
  • Bajio Region: Queretaro, San Miguel de Allende, Guanajuato (city center only)
  • Oaxaca City: Historic center and surrounding neighborhoods
  • Puerto Vallarta: Zona Romantica, Marina Vallarta

üö´ HIGH RISK ZONES (Avoid)

  • Guerrero: Including Acapulco (ongoing cartel warfare)
  • Michoacan: Except Morelia with extreme caution
  • Tamaulipas: All border cities
  • Sinaloa: Including Culiacan (cartel heartland)
  • Zacatecas: Major cartel conflict zone

Mexico City: Nuanced Risk

CDMX requires neighborhood-level analysis:

  • Safe: Roma Norte, Condesa, Polanco, Coyoacan (daylight), San Angel
  • Caution: Juarez, Santa Fe, parts of Centro Historico
  • Avoid: Iztapalapa, Tepito, peripheral areas
Emerging Threat: Increasing reports of express kidnappings (secuestro express) targeting Uber/taxi passengers in CDMX. Use only authorized rideshare apps with verified drivers.

Practical Security Measures

  • Avoid displaying wealth (expensive laptops, jewelry, cameras in public)
  • Don't work in cafes after dark
  • Use WeWork/coworking spaces instead of public WiFi for sensitive work
  • Register with your embassy's STEP program
  • Have emergency contacts saved offline
  • Keep photocopies of passport/visa separate from originals

üíº Economic & Business Environment

Currency & Cost of Living

Mexican Peso (MXN): Currently trading at approximately 17-18 MXN per USD (November 2025). The peso has strengthened slightly due to nearshoring investment flows.

Opportunity: Mexico is attracting billions in manufacturing investment due to "nearshoring" - companies moving operations from Asia. This creates stable economic outlook but also drives inflation in manufacturing hubs.

Banking & Financial Access

  • Opening bank accounts: Increasingly difficult without temporary resident visa
  • Wise/Revolut: Widely used by digital nomads for everyday transactions
  • Cash still king: Many small businesses don't accept cards; ATM access essential
  • Crypto adoption: Growing but not yet mainstream; useful for receiving international payments

Internet Infrastructure

  • Major cities: 20-100 Mbps typical, fiber available in CDMX, Monterrey, Guadalajara
  • Tourist zones: Adequate but inconsistent (10-30 Mbps)
  • Power outages: Occasional in summer (AC load); have backup power bank
  • Best providers: Telmex (ubiquitous), Izzi (faster in cities), Totalplay (fiber)

üìã Legal & Regulatory Considerations

Visa Options

1. Tourist Visa (FMM)

  • Duration: Up to 180 days (at immigration officer's discretion)
  • Work status: Technically prohibits working, even remotely
  • Current reality: Enforcement increasing; risky for long-term

2. Temporary Resident Visa (Residente Temporal)

  • Duration: 1-4 years (renewable)
  • Requirements: Prove monthly income of ~$2,800 USD or savings of ~$47,000 USD
  • Benefits: Legal right to work remotely, open bank accounts, import car
  • Process: Apply at Mexican consulate in your home country

3. Digital Nomad Visa (Pending Launch - Q2 2025)

  • Expected requirements: Remote employment proof, minimum income threshold
  • Anticipated duration: 1 year, renewable
  • Application process: Still being finalized
Tax Implications: If you spend 183+ days in Mexico, you become a tax resident. Consult with a cross-border tax specialist. Mexico has tax treaties with many countries to avoid double taxation.

Working Legally

  • Working remotely for foreign company: Temporary resident visa required
  • Working for Mexican company: Work permit required
  • Freelancing for Mexican clients: Requires RFC (tax ID) and proper visa

üåé Geopolitical Context

U.S.-Mexico Relations

Critical for understanding your environment:

  • Trade: USMCA (formerly NAFTA) remains stable; Mexico is now U.S.'s #1 trading partner
  • Migration: Contentious issue; Mexican cooperation on border enforcement affects bilateral mood
  • Nearshoring: Strengthening economic ties as companies move from China to Mexico
  • Security cooperation: Ongoing but sensitive; U.S. DEA operations in Mexico politically fraught

Regional Dynamics

  • Growing assertiveness in Central America policy
  • Strengthening ties with China (economic, not military)
  • Climate change creating migration pressures from south
Monitor: U.S. elections and policy changes can rapidly affect Mexico's internal politics and treatment of American residents. Stay informed on bilateral relations.

üéØ Recommendations by Profile

First-Time Digital Nomad (1-3 months)

  • Best locations: Playa del Carmen, Puerto Vallarta, Oaxaca
  • Visa approach: Tourist visa sufficient (avoid mentioning "work")
  • Budget: $1,500-2,500/month
  • Priority: Join coworking space immediately for community

Long-Term Remote Worker (6+ months)

  • Best locations: Mexico City, Guadalajara, Merida, Queretaro
  • Visa approach: Apply for temporary resident visa NOW; wait for digital nomad visa details
  • Budget: $2,000-3,500/month
  • Priority: Establish legal presence, learn Spanish, integrate into local community

Families with Children

  • Best locations: San Miguel de Allende, Merida, Puerto Vallarta
  • Visa approach: Temporary resident visa essential (for schooling, stability)
  • Budget: $3,500-5,000/month (with private schooling)
  • Priority: International schools (limited availability, plan early), stable housing, expat community support
